# CivicRadar

**A free community hazard map that helps neighbours in Mumbai, Pune, and Thane report monsoon stagnant water before it breeds dengue and malaria.**

---

## The problem

Every monsoon, water pools in gutters, construction pits, blocked drains, and abandoned plots across Mumbai, Pune, and Thane. Within days, that stagnant water becomes a breeding ground for the mosquitoes that spread dengue and malaria. Residents often know exactly where the trouble spots are — but reporting them is hard. A complaint phoned in or filed alone disappears into a queue with no visible status, neighbours can't see what's already been flagged, the same puddle gets reported five times or not at all, and civic teams lack the ground-level, location-specific data they need to prioritise. The risk is concrete and human: a child's fever traced back to a puddle nobody acted on in time.

## What CivicRadar does

CivicRadar is a free mobile web app (PWA — "Add to Home Screen," no app store needed) that turns scattered local knowledge into a shared, visible map. A resident photographs a stagnant-water spot; GPS automatically detects their ward; the report appears on a live ward map everyone nearby can see. Neighbours confirm the same hazard with **"Me too,"** building a corroborated signal instead of duplicate noise. When someone wants the official clock running, CivicRadar links them to file with their corporation (BMC, PMC, or TMC). It is an independent community tool — **not an official BMC, PMC, or TMC product.**

## How it works

**Citizen** — Open the app → snap a photo of stagnant water → GPS auto-detects your ward → submit in about 30 seconds → see your report on the shared ward map → neighbours tap **"Me too"** to confirm → optionally file with BMC / PMC / TMC and track the complaint number → follow the spot until it's marked **Fixed.**

**Community** — A shared ward map shows what's open near you, a leaderboard adds friendly ward-versus-ward momentum, and "Me too" corroboration turns individual reports into collective signal you can share with neighbours over WhatsApp.

**Official / NGO** — Invited BMC officials get a hazard queue that can be sorted by most-confirmed ("Most Me too") spots. NGO coordinators get a hub to manage pledges, log volunteer cleanup hours, and organise ward or neighbourhood teams.

## How it helps each stakeholder

- **Citizens** — Fast, free, and no bureaucracy. No login required, fully anonymous, works on basic phones, and available in **English, Hindi, Marathi, and Gujarati.**
- **BMC / civic bodies** — Geo-tagged, ward-level, community-corroborated hazard data; fewer duplicate complaints; an early ground-level signal of dengue risk.
- **Communities & NGOs** — A tool for collective action and transparency, with volunteer coordination and ward-level visibility into what's open and what's been fixed.

## Why now

The monsoon window is short and the public-health stakes are high. Vector-borne disease climbs every June–September, and the cheapest intervention is acting on stagnant water early. CivicRadar runs on low-cost, low-bandwidth civic tech that works on the phones people already own — so a neighbourhood can start helping itself today.
